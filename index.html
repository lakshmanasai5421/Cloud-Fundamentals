DOMAIN 01 / 18

Introduction to Cloud Computing

What cloud computing is, where it came from, and why it matters.

Cloud computing
The delivery of computing resources — servers, storage, databases, networking, software — over the internet, on demand, billed by usage.
Evolution of computing
The path from mainframes to client-server to virtualization to cloud. Each step moved compute further from the user's own hardware and closer to a shared, remote pool.
On-demand self-service
Users provision resources themselves through a portal or API, with no human interaction on the provider's side.
Broad network access
Resources are reachable over the network from any standard device — laptop, phone, or server.
Resource pooling
The provider's hardware is shared across many customers (multi-tenancy), with resources dynamically assigned as needed.
Rapid elasticity
Capacity can expand or shrink quickly — often automatically — to match demand.
Measured service
Usage is metered (CPU-hours, GB stored, requests) and billed accordingly; you pay only for what you use.
Cloud vs. on-premises
With on-prem you buy, house, and maintain your own hardware (capital expense, slow to scale); with cloud you rent capacity (operating expense, scales in minutes).
Benefits
No upfront hardware cost, near-instant global reach, elastic capacity, managed services, and faster time to market.
Limitations
Vendor lock-in, data egress charges, less low-level control, dependence on internet connectivity, compliance and residency constraints, and uncontrolled cost sprawl.
DOMAIN 02 / 18

Enabling Technologies

Virtualization, containers, and the distributed systems underneath.

Virtualization
Software that splits one physical machine into multiple isolated virtual machines. It is the foundation of resource pooling.
Hypervisor
The layer that creates and runs virtual machines. A Type 1 (bare-metal) hypervisor runs directly on hardware (ESXi, Xen, KVM); a Type 2 (hosted) hypervisor runs on top of an operating system (VirtualBox, VMware Workstation).
Virtual machine (VM)
A software-emulated computer with its own operating system, kernel, and virtual hardware. It offers strong isolation but a heavier footprint.
Containers
Lightweight packaging of an application plus its dependencies, sharing the host operating system's kernel. They start faster and pack more densely than VMs (Docker).
Container orchestration
The automated deployment, scaling, healing, and networking of containers across a cluster (Kubernetes).
Distributed systems
Multiple machines cooperating as one system — the underlying model for nearly all cloud services.
Grid, cluster & utility computing
The predecessors of cloud: pooling machines for large jobs (grid and cluster) and billing compute like a utility (utility computing).
Web services & REST APIs
The standardized HTTP interfaces through which every cloud resource is created, queried, and destroyed programmatically.
Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA)
A design style of composing systems from loosely coupled, independently deployable services — the conceptual ancestor of microservices.
DOMAIN 03 / 18

Service Models

IaaS, PaaS, SaaS, serverless, and who is responsible for what.

IaaS — Infrastructure as a Service
The provider supplies raw compute, storage, and networking, while you manage the operating system, runtime, and applications. Examples: AWS EC2, Azure VMs, Google Compute Engine.
PaaS — Platform as a Service
The provider manages the operating system, runtime, and scaling, while you supply only application code and data. Examples: Heroku, Google App Engine, AWS Elastic Beanstalk.
SaaS — Software as a Service
Fully managed applications consumed over the web, where you manage only your data and configuration. Examples: Gmail, Salesforce, Microsoft 365.
FaaS / Serverless
You deploy individual functions that run on demand, and the provider handles servers, scaling, and idle capacity. You are billed per invocation and runtime. Examples: AWS Lambda, Azure Functions.
XaaS — Anything as a Service
An umbrella term for the growing catalog of cloud-delivered offerings: databases (DBaaS), containers (CaaS), desktops or data (DaaS), storage (STaaS), and so on.
Shared Responsibility Model
The split of security duties: the provider secures the cloud (hardware, facilities, hypervisor), and you secure what you put in it (data, access, configuration). The higher up the stack you go, from IaaS to SaaS, the less falls on you.
DOMAIN 04 / 18

Deployment Models

Public, private, hybrid, community, and multi-cloud.

Public cloud
Infrastructure owned by a provider and shared across many tenants over the public internet. It is the cheapest and most elastic option, with the least control.
Private cloud
Cloud infrastructure dedicated to one organization, hosted on-premises or by a third party. It offers more control and easier compliance at a higher cost.
Hybrid cloud
A combination of private and public, with workloads and data able to move between them. Common for keeping regulated data private while bursting into public capacity.
Community cloud
Shared infrastructure for organizations with common requirements, such as government agencies or healthcare consortia.
Multi-cloud
The deliberate use of two or more public providers — to avoid lock-in, optimize cost, or meet regional requirements.
Selection criteria
Choosing a model weighs cost, compliance and data residency, latency, existing skills, workload sensitivity, and expected growth.
DOMAIN 05 / 18

Cloud Architecture

Regions, availability zones, actors, and design principles.

Reference architecture
A layered model (infrastructure, then platform, then application) plus the actors around it, used as a shared blueprint.
Cloud actors
The participants in the ecosystem: the consumer (uses services), the provider (offers them), the broker (intermediates or bundles them), the carrier (provides connectivity), and the auditor (independently assesses them).
Front end & back end
The front end is the client-side interfaces (browser, app, CLI); the back end is the provider's servers, storage, and services.
Region
A geographic area containing multiple data centers, chosen for latency, cost, and legal residency.
Availability Zone (AZ)
One or more isolated data centers within a region, each with independent power and cooling. Spreading across zones survives a single-facility failure.
Edge location (PoP)
A small site close to users, used by CDNs and edge services to cut latency.
Design for failure
The principle of assuming every component will fail and building in redundancy, retries, timeouts, and health checks from the start.
Loose coupling
Components interact through well-defined interfaces or queues, so one can fail or change without breaking the others.
Statelessness
Servers keep no client session data locally, so any request can go to any instance — a prerequisite for horizontal scaling.
DOMAIN 06 / 18

Compute

Instances, images, scaling strategies, and serverless execution.

Virtual machine instance
A rented virtual server, sized by vCPU, RAM, and storage.
Instance types / families
Preset hardware profiles tuned for a purpose: general-purpose, compute-optimized, memory-optimized, storage-optimized, and GPU/accelerated.
Machine image
A snapshot template (operating system plus preinstalled software) used to launch identical instances, such as an AMI.
Vertical scaling (scale up)
Moving to a bigger instance with more CPU and RAM. It is simple but has a hard ceiling and usually requires a restart.
Horizontal scaling (scale out)
Adding more instances behind a load balancer. It is effectively unlimited but requires stateless design.
Auto-scaling
Automatically adding or removing instances based on metrics (CPU, queue depth) or a schedule.
Load balancing
Distributing incoming traffic across healthy instances and removing failed ones from rotation.
Elasticity vs. scalability
Elasticity is the automatic short-term flex up and down with demand; scalability is the system's ability to grow to handle much larger loads over time.
Serverless compute
No instances to manage at all: code runs in response to events and scales to zero when idle.
DOMAIN 07 / 18

Storage

Object, block, and file storage — plus durability and consistency.

Object storage
Flat storage of files ("objects") with metadata, accessed over an HTTP API. Cheap, effectively infinite, and ideal for media, backups, and logs. Amazon S3 is the classic example.
Block storage
Raw virtual disks attached to a single instance and formatted with a filesystem. Low latency; used for operating-system disks and databases. Amazon EBS is an example.
File storage
A shared network filesystem (NFS or SMB) mountable by many instances at once. Amazon EFS is an example.
Storage classes / tiering
Price-versus-access tradeoffs: hot (frequent access, costly), cool (infrequent), and archive (cheapest, with retrieval taking minutes to hours).
Lifecycle policies
Rules that automatically move or delete data as it ages, for example shifting it from hot to archive after 90 days.
Durability
The probability that data is never lost, achieved by replicating it across devices and zones (often quoted as "eleven nines").
Availability
The probability that data is retrievable right now — a separate guarantee from durability.
Snapshots & backups
Point-in-time copies of volumes or databases used for recovery and cloning.
Consistency models
Strong consistency means every read returns the latest write; eventual consistency means replicas converge over time and a read may briefly be stale.
CAP theorem
In a distributed system experiencing a network partition, you must choose between consistency and availability — you cannot have all three simultaneously.
DOMAIN 08 / 18

Networking

Virtual networks, load balancers, DNS, CDNs, and egress cost.

Virtual Private Cloud (VPC)
Your own logically isolated network inside the provider's cloud, with a private IP range you control.
Subnet
A subdivision of a VPC. Public subnets have a route to the internet; private subnets do not.
Route table
The set of rules that decide where network traffic from a subnet is sent.
Internet & NAT gateways
An internet gateway grants inbound and outbound internet access; a NAT gateway lets private instances reach out without being reachable from outside.
Load balancer
Distributes traffic across targets and operates either at Layer 4 (TCP) or Layer 7 (HTTP, with routing rules).
DNS
Maps domain names to IP addresses; cloud DNS services also handle health-based and geographic routing. Route 53 is an example.
CDN — Content Delivery Network
Caches content at edge locations near users to cut latency and origin load. CloudFront is an example.
VPN & Direct Connect
A VPN is an encrypted tunnel over the internet; Direct Connect (or ExpressRoute) is a dedicated private circuit linking on-premises to the cloud.
Peering
A private connection between two VPCs (or two networks) so they can communicate without traversing the internet.
Security group
A stateful virtual firewall attached to an instance, supporting allow-rules only.
Network ACL
A stateless firewall at the subnet boundary, supporting both allow and deny rules.
Bandwidth, latency & throughput
Bandwidth is the maximum capacity, latency is the delay per request, and throughput is the data actually moved per second.
Data egress cost
The charge for data leaving the provider's network. Inbound is usually free, but outbound is a major — and often overlooked — cost driver.
DOMAIN 09 / 18

Databases in the Cloud

Relational, the four NoSQL families, warehouses, and lakes.

Managed relational database
A hosted RDBMS (MySQL, PostgreSQL, SQL Server) where the provider handles patching, backups, and failover. Amazon RDS is an example.
NoSQL — key-value
Stores that handle simple key-to-value lookups at massive scale and very low latency. DynamoDB and Redis are examples.
NoSQL — document
Stores that hold semi-structured, JSON-like documents with flexible schemas. MongoDB and Cosmos DB are examples.
NoSQL — column-family
Wide-column stores optimized for huge volumes and fast writes. Cassandra and HBase are examples.
NoSQL — graph
Stores that hold nodes and relationships and are efficient for connected data such as social graphs and fraud rings. Neptune is an example.
Data warehouse
An analytics-optimized store for structured data, using columnar formats and massively parallel queries. Redshift, BigQuery, and Snowflake are examples.
Data lake
A central repository holding raw data of any format, structured and unstructured, usually on object storage, with schema applied at read time.
Caching layer
An in-memory store (Redis, Memcached) placed in front of a database to serve hot data in microseconds and reduce load.
Read replica
A read-only copy of a database that offloads query traffic from the primary.
Sharding / partitioning
Splitting data horizontally across nodes so each holds a subset — the standard way to scale writes.
Replication
Copying data to other nodes or regions for availability, durability, and low-latency local reads.
DOMAIN 10 / 18

Cloud Security

Identity, encryption, common threats, and compliance frameworks.

IAM — Identity & Access Management
The system defining who can do what to which resource. It is built from users, groups, roles, and policies.
Role
A set of permissions that can be assumed temporarily by a user or a service — the preferred alternative to long-lived credentials.
Policy
A document (usually JSON) that explicitly allows or denies specific actions on specific resources.
Principle of least privilege
Granting only the minimum permissions needed for a task, and nothing more.
Authentication vs. authorization
Authentication is proving who you are; authorization is determining what you are allowed to do.
MFA — Multi-Factor Authentication
Requires a second factor, such as an app code or hardware key, in addition to a password.
SSO & federation
Sign in once with a central identity provider — such as a corporate directory — and access many cloud accounts and apps.
Encryption at rest
Data is stored encrypted on disk, so stolen media is unreadable.
Encryption in transit
Data is encrypted while moving over the network, using TLS/HTTPS.
Key management (KMS/HSM)
The central creation, storage, rotation, and access-control of encryption keys; hardware security modules provide hardware-backed protection.
Misconfiguration
The single most common cause of cloud breaches — for example, a storage bucket accidentally left public.
DDoS
Flooding a service with traffic to knock it offline, mitigated with scrubbing services and edge protection such as AWS Shield.
Account hijacking & insider threats
Stolen credentials or abuse by authorized users, countered with MFA, least privilege, and audit logging.
Compliance frameworks
Rulesets a workload must satisfy: GDPR (EU privacy), HIPAA (US health data), PCI-DSS (payment cards), ISO 27001 (information-security management), and SOC 2 (controls reporting).
Data sovereignty / residency
The legal requirement that data be stored and processed within specific national borders, which drives region choice.
DOMAIN 11 / 18

Virtualization & Resource Management

Multi-tenancy, provisioning, scheduling, and quality of service.

Multi-tenancy
Many customers sharing the same physical hardware while remaining logically isolated from one another.
Resource pooling
Aggregating CPU, memory, and storage into a shared pool that is allocated dynamically on demand.
Provisioning
Allocating and configuring resources for a workload — manually, automatically, or through full self-service.
Scheduling / workload placement
Deciding which physical host runs a given VM or container, balancing utilization, affinity, and isolation.
Over-provisioning & oversubscription
Selling more virtual capacity than physical capacity, on the bet that not all tenants peak at once.
Live migration
Moving a running VM between physical hosts with little or no downtime, used for maintenance and rebalancing.
QoS — Quality of Service
The guarantees or limits on the resources — IOPS, bandwidth, CPU — a tenant receives.
Noisy neighbor
The performance degradation caused by another tenant on shared hardware consuming excessive resources.
DOMAIN 12 / 18

Reliability & Availability

High availability, disaster recovery, RTO/RPO, and SLAs.

High availability (HA)
Designing so the service keeps running through component failures, typically via redundancy across zones.
Fault tolerance
A stronger property: the system continues at full capability even when components fail, with no degradation.
Redundancy
Duplicate components — instances, zones, regions — so no single failure takes the system down.
Single point of failure (SPOF)
Any component whose failure alone brings down the whole system; eliminating it is the point of HA design.
Disaster recovery (DR)
The plan and tooling to restore service after a major outage. Strategies range from backup and restore, to pilot light, to warm standby, to multi-site active/active, with increasing cost and decreasing downtime.
RTO — Recovery Time Objective
The maximum acceptable time to restore service after an outage.
RPO — Recovery Point Objective
The maximum acceptable amount of data loss, measured in time — for example, "no more than five minutes of data."
SLA, SLO & SLI
The SLA is the contractual uptime promise and its penalties, the SLO is the internal target, and the SLI is the actual measured indicator.
The "nines"
Uptime shorthand: 99.9% is roughly 8.8 hours of downtime per year, 99.99% is about 53 minutes per year, and 99.999% is about 5 minutes per year.
DOMAIN 13 / 18

Cloud Economics

Pricing models, total cost of ownership, and FinOps.

CapEx to OpEx
Cloud converts big upfront capital purchases into ongoing operational spend that tracks actual usage.
On-demand pricing
Paying by the second or hour with no commitment. It is the most flexible option and the highest unit price.
Reserved instances / savings plans
Committing to one to three years of usage for a large discount, often 40 to 70 percent.
Spot instances
Bidding on spare capacity at a steep discount, accepting that it can be reclaimed with little notice. Suited to fault-tolerant batch jobs.
Pay-as-you-go
The underlying billing philosophy: you are charged for consumption, not capacity.
TCO — Total Cost of Ownership
The full lifetime cost including hardware, power, cooling, staff, licensing, and downtime, used to compare cloud and on-premises honestly.
Cost drivers
Compute hours, storage volume and class, data egress, requests and API calls, managed-service premiums, and idle or orphaned resources.
Cost optimization
Rightsizing instances, deleting unused resources, tiering storage, using commitments and spot capacity, and turning off dev/test environments outside working hours.
FinOps
The practice of shared cost accountability across engineering, finance, and product, using tagging, budgets, and showback or chargeback.
DOMAIN 14 / 18

Cloud Management & Operations

Observability, infrastructure as code, and CI/CD.

Monitoring
Collecting metrics — CPU, latency, error rate — to know how a system is behaving.
Logging
Capturing detailed event records for debugging, security, and audit.
Alerting
Automatically notifying humans or systems when a metric crosses a threshold.
Observability
The ability to understand a system's internal state from its outputs — metrics, logs, and traces together.
Infrastructure as Code (IaC)
Defining infrastructure in version-controlled config files so environments are reproducible and reviewable. Terraform, CloudFormation, and Pulumi are examples.
Declarative vs. imperative
Declarative IaC states the desired end state and lets the tool reach it; imperative scripts state the steps.
Idempotency
Running the same operation repeatedly produces the same result — essential for safe automation.
CI/CD
Continuous Integration (automatic build and test on every commit) and Continuous Delivery or Deployment (automatic release to environments).
DevOps
The culture and practices merging development and operations to ship faster with shared ownership of reliability.
Configuration management
Keeping servers in a known, consistent state with tools such as Ansible, Chef, and Puppet.
Governance & tagging
Applying metadata tags and organizational policies (guardrails) to control cost, ownership, and compliance at scale.
Immutable infrastructure
Never patching a running server but instead replacing it with a new image, eliminating configuration drift.
DOMAIN 15 / 18

Migration to the Cloud

Assessment, the six R's, and avoiding vendor lock-in.

Assessment / discovery
Inventorying existing applications, dependencies, and data to decide what moves, and in what order.
Rehost ("lift and shift")
Moving the workload as-is to cloud VMs. The fastest approach with the least benefit.
Replatform ("lift, tinker & shift")
Making small cloud-friendly optimizations — such as swapping a self-managed database for a managed one — without rewriting.
Refactor / re-architect
Redesigning the application for the cloud (microservices, serverless). The highest cost and the highest payoff.
Repurchase
Dropping the app and buying a SaaS equivalent.
Retire
Decommissioning workloads that are no longer needed.
Retain
Deliberately leaving a workload on-premises for now, usually due to compliance or dependency constraints.
Migration phases
From assess, to plan, to migrate (piloting first), to validate, to optimize.
Vendor lock-in
Dependence on one provider's proprietary services, which makes a future move costly. Mitigated with open standards, containers, IaC, and abstraction layers.
Portability & interoperability
Portability is the ability to move workloads between clouds; interoperability is having systems in different clouds work together.
DOMAIN 16 / 18

Major Cloud Providers

AWS, Azure, GCP, and how their terminology maps.

AWS — Amazon Web Services
The largest and broadest provider and the earliest to market. Core services include EC2, S3, VPC, RDS, Lambda, and IAM.
Microsoft Azure
Strong in enterprise and hybrid scenarios, integrating tightly with Windows, Active Directory, and Microsoft 365. Core services include Azure VMs, Blob Storage, VNet, Azure SQL, and Functions.
Google Cloud Platform (GCP)
Strengths in data, analytics, and Kubernetes (which Google originated). Core services include Compute Engine, Cloud Storage, VPC, BigQuery, Cloud Functions, and GKE.
Other providers
Oracle Cloud (databases), IBM Cloud (hybrid and enterprise), Alibaba Cloud (Asia-Pacific), and DigitalOcean or Linode for smaller-scale simplicity.
Terminology mapping
The same concepts wear different names: a VM is EC2, an Azure VM, or a Compute Engine instance; an object store is S3, Blob Storage, or Cloud Storage; serverless is Lambda, Azure Functions, or Cloud Functions.
DOMAIN 17 / 18

Emerging & Advanced Topics

Edge, cloud-native, service mesh, AI/ML, and confidential computing.

Edge computing
Processing data near where it is generated — in stores, factories, or vehicles — instead of in a distant region, cutting latency and bandwidth.
Fog computing
An intermediate layer between edge devices and the cloud that aggregates and preprocesses data.
Cloud-native
Building specifically for the cloud: containers, microservices, declarative APIs, and automation, designed for elasticity and failure.
Microservices
Structuring an application as small, independently deployable services, each owning one capability.
API gateway
A managed front door for APIs, handling routing, authentication, rate limiting, and throttling.
Service mesh
An infrastructure layer (such as Istio) that handles service-to-service traffic: mutual TLS, retries, routing, and observability.
Big data & analytics
Cloud-scale processing of large datasets (Spark, Hadoop, streaming pipelines) using elastic, on-demand clusters.
AI/ML services
Managed model training, hosting, and prebuilt APIs (vision, speech, language) available without owning GPUs.
IoT & cloud
Ingesting, storing, and analyzing telemetry from large fleets of connected devices.
Green computing / sustainability
Using efficient hardware, high utilization, and renewable-powered regions to reduce the carbon footprint per workload.
Confidential computing
Encrypting data in use by processing it inside hardware-protected enclaves (trusted execution environments).
Well-Architected principles
Provider frameworks that evaluate a workload across pillars: operational excellence, security, reliability, performance efficiency, cost optimization, and sustainability.
DOMAIN 18 / 18

Legal, Ethical & Standards

Privacy law, contracts, certification, and open standards.

Privacy & data protection law
Rules governing the collection and processing of personal data (GDPR, CCPA, India's DPDP Act), with real penalties for breaches.
Cloud contracts & SLAs
The agreements defining uptime guarantees, liability, support tiers, and remedies (usually service credits rather than damages).
Auditing & certification
Independent verification that a provider or workload meets a standard (SOC 2, ISO 27001), often required by enterprise customers.
Right to audit & transparency
The customer's contractual ability to review the provider's controls, and the provider's obligation to disclose incidents.
Open standards & interoperability
Common formats and APIs (OCI containers, Kubernetes, S3-compatible APIs) that reduce lock-in and let workloads move.
Ethical considerations
Data ownership, surveillance capability, environmental impact, and equitable access to compute.
